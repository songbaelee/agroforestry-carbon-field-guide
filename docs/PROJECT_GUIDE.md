# Agroforestry Carbon Field Guide

## Project Guide (Version 0.2)

> This document captures the guiding principles for the project. It is
> the source of truth for editorial, research, and design decisions
> expected to remain stable over time.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 1. Mission

The Agroforestry Carbon Field Guide exists to help readers understand
the organizations, business models, and relationships that make up the
agroforestry carbon ecosystem.

The site is intended to teach the ecosystem, not simply catalog
organizations.

# 2. Audience

-   Climate finance practitioners
-   Agroforestry project developers
-   Development finance professionals
-   Corporate sustainability teams
-   Researchers and students

Assume readers are intelligent but may be unfamiliar with the structure
of the market.

# 3. Design Philosophy

The website is a **Field Guide**, not a company directory.

Every page should help readers answer:

1.  What does this organization do?
2.  Where does it fit?
3.  What organizations are commonly confused with it?
4.  How is it different?
5.  Where can I learn more?

The objective is orientation first, detail second.

# 4. Organization Profile Standard

1.  Hero
2.  Primary Role
3.  Closest Adjacent Organizations
4.  Featured Comparison
5.  Research Profile
6.  Common Collaborators
7.  References

# 5. Ecosystem Philosophy

Organizations are organized by ecosystem role rather than popularity.

Featured comparisons are editorial choices designed to teach, not rank.

Closest Adjacent Organizations identify organizations readers are most
likely to confuse.

# 6. Editorial Standards

-   Hero summary: 75--125 words.
-   Ecosystem Fit: 2--3 concise sentences.
-   Adjacent comparison: 1--2 concise sentences.
-   Narrative sections use short paragraphs with blank lines.
-   Representative projects use bullets whenever practical.
-   References prioritize official sources.

# 7. Research Standards

-   Official sources first.
-   Never invent information.
-   Distinguish documented facts from inference.
-   Preserve existing fields when expanding profiles.

# 8. Technical Workflow

1.  Research one organization.
2.  Validate JSON.
3.  Review the diff.
4.  Commit one profile per commit.
5.  Review the live page.
6.  Update this guide whenever standards change.

# 9. Living Document

This guide should evolve with the project. When a new standard is
adopted, document it here so future contributors and coding assistants
inherit the project's philosophy.

# 10. Profile Editorial Review

## Comparisons

Featured Comparisons should teach. Explain why the organizations are
being compared, how their ecosystem roles, business models, and
financing approaches differ, and what readers learn from the
comparison.

Avoid comparisons that simply state that two organizations are similar.

## Ecosystem Fit

Every completed profile should explain where the organization sits
within the ecosystem, which functions it performs, which functions it
does not perform, and how it connects to other participants.

Readers should understand the relationships among buyers, developers,
financiers, registries, standards, MRV providers, technical assistance
organizations, and farmers.

## Business Model

Business Model should explain how the organization creates value, why
it participates in agroforestry, and whether carbon is the product, an
enabling mechanism, a financing tool, or simply an outcome.

Avoid language implying that every organization is fundamentally a
carbon company.

## Financing Approach

Financing Approach should describe financial flows rather than simply
mention financing. Where possible, explain who provides and receives
capital, how incentives flow, where carbon revenue fits, and whether
project economics depend primarily on carbon or broader commercial
objectives.

Readers should understand the financial mechanics without needing
outside references.

## Representative Projects

Representative projects should illustrate strategy rather than merely
list initiatives. Whenever possible, select examples that demonstrate
geographic diversity, implementation approach, financing model,
partnerships, measurable scale, and strategic importance.

The examples should reinforce the organization's business model.

## Sources

Continue prioritizing primary sources. Whenever practical, supplement
official sources with at least one reputable independent source that
provides additional context or validation.

## Similarity Scores

Similarity scores should be calibrated across the entire directory.
Individual profiles should not be optimized independently when doing so
reduces consistency across comparable organizations.

Perform periodic cross-profile reviews to maintain scoring consistency.
