# Agroforestry Carbon Field Guide

A field guide to the organizations, roles, and relationships that make up the agroforestry carbon ecosystem.

This project is designed to help readers understand not only who the major actors are, but how they fit together: project developers, standards bodies, registries, financiers, corporate buyers, technical assistance providers, MRV providers, and other participants in the market.

Live site: https://songbaelee.github.io/agroforestry-carbon-field-guide/

## Why this project exists

I like learning by building.

After several years focused on agricultural finance, I began working more directly on agroforestry carbon finance. The subject combined familiar themes, including smallholder agriculture, investment, development finance, and technical assistance, with a much less familiar ecosystem of carbon standards, registries, project developers, corporate buyers, and verification systems.

This project grew out of a sequence of learning tools:

| Project | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Agroforestry Carbon Finance Learning Tool | How do agroforestry carbon projects work? |
| TechnoServe archetype model | Do I really understand the economics behind the published research? |
| Agroforestry Carbon Field Guide | Who are the participants, and how does the ecosystem function? |

The Field Guide is the next step in that progression. It focuses less on the mechanics of a single project and more on the ecosystem around agroforestry carbon finance.

## What this is

This is a field guide, not a company directory.

A directory lists organizations. A field guide helps readers recognize patterns, distinguish similar actors, and understand relationships.

Each organization profile is designed to answer:

- What does this organization do?
- Where does it fit in the ecosystem?
- What other organizations is it commonly confused with?
- How is it different?
- Where can readers learn more?

The goal is orientation first, detail second.

## Who this is for

This resource is for people who want to learn more about climate finance, carbon finance, and the specific challenges of applying carbon finance to agroforestry.

It is especially intended for readers who are on a similar learning journey: people who may understand pieces of agriculture, finance, development, sustainability, or carbon markets, but want a clearer mental model of how the agroforestry carbon ecosystem fits together.

It assumes curiosity, not prior expertise in every part of the market.

## Why these 19 organizations?

The first version of the Field Guide includes 19 organizations.

That number is not intended to be exhaustive, and the list is not a ranking of importance. ChatGPT proposed the initial set of 19 organizations during the research and design process based on ecosystem coverage, educational value, and the need to represent the major roles involved in agroforestry carbon finance.

The selection emphasizes educational coverage: organizations were included because, together, they help illustrate the different functions, relationships, and business models that shape agroforestry carbon finance.

## How the Field Guide is organized

The current version focuses on an Organizations Directory and an Ecosystem Map.

Organization profiles include:

- a concise summary
- primary ecosystem role
- closest adjacent organizations
- featured comparison
- research profile
- common collaborators
- references

Featured comparisons are editorial choices. They are not rankings. Their purpose is to help readers understand how similar-looking organizations differ in role, business model, financing approach, or ecosystem position.

## How this project is built

This project is also an experiment in learning and building with AI.

The editorial direction comes from the learning goals of the project. ChatGPT is used as a collaborative research, writing, and design partner. Codex is used to implement and refine the website, translate editorial decisions into code, and improve the user experience.

The process is iterative:

1. define the learning question
2. research the topic
3. organize the material into a usable structure
4. draft or revise content
5. implement changes in the website
6. review the result
7. refine again

The goal is not to automate judgment. The goal is to use AI to accelerate a human-led learning process.
